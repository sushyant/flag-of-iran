History of Iran’s flag

The Lion and Sun is one of Iran’s national symbols that was officially used until the 1979 Revolution. A common misconception is that the Lion and Sun flag is the emblem of the Pahlavi monarchy. Although this flag was also used during the Pahlavi era, it is an ancient and national symbol whose use as a flag dates back to the sixteenth century, during the Safavid dynasty.

An interesting point is that the origins of the Lion and Sun symbol go back to the pre-Islamic era. In pre-Islamic Iran, the sun was a symbol of Mithra and was rooted in the beliefs of ancient Iranians. The sun is mentioned many times in the Avesta and the Gathas. The sun (Xwaršēd in Middle Persian) is a divinity about whom it is stated in the sixth Yasht of the Avesta that he keeps the world free from pollution.

The sun, often represented in human form, has long been associated with kingship in Iran. The “crystalline image of the sun” distinguished the royal pavilion of Darius III. The Parthian standard was adorned with an image of the sun, and the crowns of the Sasanian shahanshahs bore a globe symbolising the sun. John Malalas, the Greek historian, refers to a letter in which the king of Iran is called the “Sun of the East” and the Roman Caesar the “Moon of the West”.

The lion has consistently appeared prominently on statues, reliefs, bowls, and golden and silver cups during the Achaemenid, Parthian, and Sasanian periods. The lion was closely associated with kingship in Iran; rows of lions adorned the throne and robes of the Achaemenid kings. The crown of Antiochus I, the Persian-descended king of Commagene (r. 69–34 BC), was decorated with an image of a lion. In the rock relief depicting the investiture ceremony at Naqsh-e Rostam, Ardashir I is shown wearing armour bearing the image of a lion.
